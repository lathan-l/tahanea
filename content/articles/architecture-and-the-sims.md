# Title (work in progress)

As you already know, I am a normal Sims player, and a normal person.
Very demure, very mindful. Nothing out of the ordinary. And I like to write about The Sims games and their impact on my life.
And sometimes, stars align, and I am so thankful for Will Wright. 

Like many others, The Sims sparked in me a keen interest in architecture. Indeed, a huge part of the game consists in building a house for your Sims to live in. Actually, this was supposed to be *the game*, but playing virtual dolls had such an impact on people, things got out of hand and here we are: The Sims is not solely an architecture game. Alas! But it was supposed to be.
As I was saying, countless players discovered that designing and building houses was an amazing passtime and later decided to embrace the Architect Career. I was not one of them, but I sure thought of it.
Unfortunately, physics and maths bored me in high school, whereas art history and languages kept my grades high enough to be able to play The Sims every night without being denied the use of my personal computer. I made a deal with the devil and I have no regrets: I studied linguistics and could not be happier with my choice. What's funny is that I have become a developer, and now most of my days are spent talking about architecture - software architecture. 

As a linguist, I am interested in language in general, and in grammars and natural language generation in particular. As a computational linguistics student, one of the first things I created was a very simple and short formal grammar to generate weather reports. Here's one sentence it could write, and this is 2014 I kid you not: "Jeudi 31 février au cours de la journée il fera un froid de canard dans la maison de vos Sims." (On Thursday, February 31st it is going to be icy cold in your Sims' house.) Look at me building sentences by day, houses by night! What a nerd.

Anyway, the other day, my boyfriend told me how impressed he was at the houses I was building in The Sims. I had told him I'd build "Belgian houses" like the ones he showed me near the Ixelles ponds and he did not expect my "build" to actually look good, let alone resemble real houses. Oh honey, I've been building for 25 years: of course my houses look good! They look better than good! And I know a thing or two about architecture! But it's when I started ranting about the history of corridors, and why they were both interesting technology and some kind of pitfall in designing houses for my Sims, that it struck me: I do know a thing or two about architecture and interior design, and I have Mr. Will Wright to thank for this. Let me tell you all about it.

## How to build a house in The Sims

First of all: why build a house? If you are interested in *winning*, you could be trying to build the most *efficient* place for your sims to live. By that, I mean a place where a sim can tend to their needs in a way that maximizes their happiness and overall satisfaction. Of course, this means that the so-called house will probably resemble... a bunker. And sims need enrichment and like pretty things, so a bunker with artworks? Probably stolen, if you're cheating. A two-room art dealer mafia bunker with a tub? Something like that. 

This is not what players usually think of doing when starting to play. Well, not on purpose. Players start with a model in mind: they know what a house looks like - because everybody knows what a house looks like! And yet, everybody's house is different. As a little French player, I remember having a lot of fun imagining American houses. I tried creating the apartments and houses I knew, but the first game was very limited in terms of construction: I had to create boring rectangular suburban houses. No wonder everybody was drowning neighbours in the pool. Instead, the creative process of building fantasy houses took over. If I can't create *my* house, just let me create the most extravagant McMansion! Pools, columns, stucco, windows everywhere, statues, you name it! More on this subject later.

But what if you could actually build a realistic house to play with? I'm not going to describe the evolution of building tools throughout the games; let's just say that the original game was limited but great, The Sims 2 were 3D!!!, The Sims 3 introduced custom textures (and I'm not ready to show the ugly patterns I used from floor to ceiling, matching curtains, sofas, chairs - let me tell you, taste is not innate and mauve should be banned) and The Sims 4 is an *amazing* building game, where you can build realistic-ish houses.

What is a realistic house then? What is a house? Just a bunch of rooms, maybe a corridor or two? As a kid, and a teenager, I used a squared notebook to draw house plans. *My realistic* back then looked like an individual house (but it is a strong game limitation), usually rectangular or L-shaped, with clearly identified rooms and doors. When designing bigger houses, symmetry helped me get the shape right by stacking rooms on the side of a central room, and maybe a tower. The rooms were identified as bedrooms, bathrooms, kitchen and living room. Fancy houses had halls, hallways, atriums, studies, secret labs.
